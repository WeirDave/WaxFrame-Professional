PROJECT 1 — Blog Post on Multi-AI Document Refinement
============================================================
Target playbook entry: Blog Post / Article (we have a 16-round
run on a different topic — this gives us a second data point
on the same document type, your domain expertise behind it.)

PROJECT-SCREEN VALUES
---------------------
Project Name:    Blog — Why I Stopped Trusting Single-Model AI for Network Docs
Project Version: v1.0
Document Type:   Blog Post

Target Audience:
  Senior network engineers and IT leads who already use
  ChatGPT or Claude for technical documentation and have hit
  the wall where the output is "fine but not right." Reading
  on LinkedIn, Reddit r/networking, or a personal blog.

Desired Outcome:
  Reader finishes the post with a concrete reason to try
  running the same document through multiple models in
  sequence. They click through to the linked tool at the end.

Scope:
  Cover: the one-shot trap (single model leaves blind spots),
  why different models catch different issues, a concrete
  network-engineering example with real numbers, and what the
  reader can try this week. Do not invent statistics. No
  marketing fluff.

Tone:
  First-person, direct, slightly sarcastic. Written the way I
  would say it to another network engineer over a beer, not
  the way I would write a vendor whitepaper. Voice is someone
  who's been burned by AI-generated config drift and finally
  found a workflow that doesn't suck.

Additional Instructions (Goal Notes):
  No buzzwords. No "synergy" "leverage" "revolutionize" or
  any LinkedIn-influencer language. If a paragraph reads like
  a product brochure, cut it. Keep my voice — do not sand it
  down into something that could have been written by anyone.

Length Limit: 800 words
Source:       From scratch
Notes (per-round): leave blank, add only if voice drifts


REFERENCE MATERIAL PAYLOAD (paste into Setup 4)
-----------------------------------------------
Thesis: any single LLM, no matter how good, has blind spots.
Run the same document through 4 or 5 different models in
sequence and the output is meaningfully better than any one
model can produce on its own.

Concrete example to reference:
- Took an Aruba ArubaOS 8.x configuration document, ~900 words,
  describing an ArubaOS firmware-upgrade procedure for a
  multi-controller deployment
- Ran it through Claude alone: got a tightened version,
  maybe 15% better. Still missed two ordering issues that
  would have caused a controller failover during the upgrade
- Ran the same document through Claude → ChatGPT → Gemini →
  back to Claude: got a version where both ordering issues
  were caught, the prerequisites section was reorganized, and
  one assumption about RADIUS server reachability was flagged
  as needing verification before the change window

What the reader should try today: pick a runbook or
configuration document they care about, hand it to 3
different models in sequence with the same prompt, compare
the result against running it through any one model once.

Call to action: link to a tool that automates this — multiple
models, single document, convergence loop. (This is WaxFrame
but the post should not name it overtly — let the link speak
for itself.)

PROJECT 2 — LinkedIn Post (short-form announcement)
============================================================
Target playbook entry: NEW playbook category we don't have yet.
Short-form posts converge differently than long-form blog
posts — fewer rounds, more brutal cutting.

PROJECT-SCREEN VALUES
---------------------
Project Name:    LinkedIn Post — Hit 100 Wireless Site Surveys
Project Version: v1.0
Document Type:   LinkedIn Post

Target Audience:
  My LinkedIn network — mostly wireless engineers, IT
  managers, and a handful of recruiters. They scroll fast.
  If the first two lines don't land, they keep scrolling.

Desired Outcome:
  Reader stops scrolling, reads the whole post, and either
  comments with their own war story or sends a connection
  request. Post should feel earned, not braggy.

Scope:
  Maximum 200 words. Hook in the first line. One concrete
  lesson from doing 100 surveys, not a generic "I learned
  patience" platitude. End with a question that invites
  comments. No hashtag spam — three relevant hashtags max.

Tone:
  Conversational, dry, no LinkedIn-influencer voice. Sounds
  like a peer talking to peers, not a thought leader trying
  to grow an audience.

Additional Instructions (Goal Notes):
  Do not start with "I'm proud to announce" or "thrilled" or
  "humbled" — the LinkedIn opener virus. No emojis except
  maybe one at the end. No call to "DM me to learn more."

Length Limit: 200 words
Source:       From scratch


REFERENCE MATERIAL PAYLOAD (paste into Setup 4)
-----------------------------------------------
Milestone: I just finished my 100th wireless site survey.
Mix is roughly 60% predictive (Ekahau, iBwave) and 40%
on-site validation surveys.

The lesson I want to land:
The biggest mistake I made on my first 20 surveys was
trusting the predictive model. The map says coverage is
green at -65 dBm and you walk in and the actual signal is
-78 dBm because the predictive model didn't know about the
metal mezzanine, the racks of inventory, or the fact that
the customer's "drywall" is actually CMU block painted to
look like drywall.

Predictive surveys are a starting point. They are not the
answer. The answer comes from walking the floor with a
laptop and a real survey adapter and finding out where the
model lied to you.

Question to end on: what's the worst predictive-vs-actual
delta you've measured?

Hashtag candidates: #WirelessNetworking #SiteSurvey #CWNP

PROJECT 3 — LinkedIn Profile Section (About Me)
============================================================
Target playbook entry: NEW playbook category — profile-style
content. Different from blog or LinkedIn post: it has to
work as both a recruiter screen AND a peer impression. Two
audiences, one block of text.

PROJECT-SCREEN VALUES
---------------------
Project Name:    LinkedIn Profile — About Section
Project Version: v1.0
Document Type:   LinkedIn About Section

Target Audience:
  Two readers at the same time — (1) a recruiter scanning for
  CWNA / CWAP / Aruba / Ekahau keywords in 30 seconds, and
  (2) a peer wireless engineer who can smell a fluffy
  About section from across the room. The section has to
  work for both without choosing one.

Desired Outcome:
  Recruiter sees the credentials and platform experience and
  flags it as a match. Peer reads it and thinks "this person
  actually does the work, not just talks about it."

Scope:
  Maximum 2,000 characters (LinkedIn's hard cap). Lead with
  what I actually do, not where I work. Include credentials
  (CWNA, CWDP, CWAP) and platform experience (Aruba on-prem
  ArubaOS 8.x, Ruckus, Cisco Meraki, Ekahau, iBwave) without
  it reading like a resume bullet list. End with one line
  that gives a peer something to connect on.

Tone:
  Professional but human. Not stiff. Not performative.
  Sounds like a sentence I would actually say at a meetup,
  not the sanitized corporate version of myself.

Additional Instructions (Goal Notes):
  No "passionate about." No "results-driven." No "proven
  track record." If a sentence sounds like it could have
  been written about anyone in my field, rewrite it until
  it could only have been written about me.

Length Limit: 2000 characters
Source:       From scratch
