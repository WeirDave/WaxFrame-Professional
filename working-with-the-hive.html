Working with the Hive

Writing a Strong Project Goal
Your goal is the foundation of everything

Every AI in your hive reads your goal at the start of the Draft phase. It tells them what the document is, what it's trying to achieve, who it's for, and what direction to take. A vague goal produces a vague first draft. A specific, detailed goal produces something you can actually work with.

The first 300 characters of your goal are sent as active context in every Refine round — make sure your most important intent lands in that window. A counter next to the goal field turns amber when you pass the 300-character threshold.

What to include

What the document is — a vendor comparison, an executive summary, a proposal, a technical guide, a marketing one-pager. Be specific about the format.

The scope — what's in and what's out. If your document covers wireless networking only, say so explicitly. If it excludes routing and switching, say that too. AIs will naturally expand into adjacent topics unless you draw the boundary clearly.

The audience — a technical team, a C-suite, a customer, a prospective buyer. This shapes the language and depth every AI will use.

The outcome — what should someone be able to do or decide after reading this document?

Example: weak vs strong

Weak "Compare wireless platforms for our company."

Strong "Create a technical comparison of enterprise wireless networking platforms — HPE Aruba, Cisco Meraki, Juniper Mist, and Extreme Networks. Audience is our IT infrastructure team evaluating a campus refresh. Scope is strictly wireless: access points, controllers, cloud management, and licensing. Do not include switching, routing, or security products. The output should help the team make a shortlist recommendation."

You can always edit your goal between rounds. Changes take effect from the next round forward. If your goal was too vague and the document has already drifted, see Course Correcting Mid-Session.

How Rounds Work
Every round has two phases
  1. 1Review phase — all your active Worker Bees read the document simultaneously and return numbered suggestions. They run in parallel, so more AIs doesn't mean more time.
  2. 2Build phase — your Builder reads all the suggestions plus the full document, applies the best ones, resolves disagreements where it can, flags unresolvable conflicts for your review, and produces the updated document.
Draft vs Refine

1 · Draft Used when starting from scratch. The full goal is sent to every AI. This is when your goal matters most — the AIs are building your document from nothing.

2 · Refine Text Used for all subsequent rounds, and automatically when you upload or paste an existing document. Only the first 300 characters of your goal are sent as context. The focus shifts to improving what's already there.

The Builder does the heavy lifting

Your Builder reads the entire document plus every reviewer suggestion every single round. It uses significantly more tokens than any reviewer. On a 2,000-word document with 5 reviewers, expect roughly 8,000–12,000 tokens per Builder call. Choose an AI with enough token capacity for your document size — DeepSeek is the most cost-effective option by a wide margin.

If a round fails with a missing output structure error, try switching your Builder to ChatGPT or Gemini and retrying. Some AIs are less consistent at following strict formatting instructions on large documents.

Working with Conflicts
What is a conflict?

When your AIs disagree on something and the Builder can't confidently pick a winner, it flags it in the Conflicts panel rather than making an arbitrary call. There are two kinds:

User Decision The Builder needs you to choose. Two or more AIs suggested different approaches and neither is clearly better. This one won't proceed until you weigh in.

Builder Decision The Builder made a judgment call. It chose something and kept moving. Review what it picked — if you disagree, override it.

Your three options for every conflict

Pick an option — choose one of the AI-generated alternatives. The Builder applies it to the document immediately.

Type your own — select Custom and type the exact text you want inserted. What you type becomes the replacement text in the document — write the actual sentence or phrase you want, not an instruction. For example, type "HPE Aruba acquired Juniper Networks in 2024; this evaluation focuses on their wireless portfolio only." — not "remove the switching references."

Decline — skip this conflict and leave the current text in place.

Click the "Current:" text on any conflict card to jump directly to that line in the working document and highlight it — useful when you want to see the surrounding context before deciding.

Resolved conflicts are locked in

Once you resolve a conflict, your decision is injected into every subsequent round. The AIs won't re-raise it — the hive treats your choice as final. This prevents the same debate from surfacing round after round.

⚠️ Grok in particular has a tendency to re-raise resolved points due to a confidence bias. The resolved decisions system handles this, but if Grok keeps looping on settled decisions in later rounds, toggle it off — its value is highest in early rounds anyway.

Using conflicts to steer scope

Conflicts aren't just for tiebreaking — they're one of your most direct tools for steering the document mid-session. If a conflict surfaces content that's out of scope, use the Custom option to replace it with something tighter, or Decline to leave the current text and suppress the suggestion. Either way, the decision gets locked and the AIs won't push it again.

Course Correcting Mid-Session
What to do when the document drifts

You're a few rounds in and you realise the hive has been going in the wrong direction — scope crept in, the tone is off, or your goal wasn't specific enough and the AIs filled in the gaps their way. This happens. Here's how to fix it.

Option A — Fix the goal + use a Note (lightest touch)

Go back to the Project screen via the nav menu and tighten your goal — add explicit scope boundaries, tone direction, or anything that was missing. Then before the next round, add a Note that gives the Builder immediate corrective instructions.

Example note: "Do not include routing, switching, or security products. Wireless components only. Remove any switching references already in the document."

The updated goal sets the direction going forward. The note targets the Builder's next pass specifically. Together they're your fastest path to correction.

Option B — Fix the goal + edit the document directly

The working document is editable at any time between rounds. If you know exactly what needs to come out, remove it yourself, tighten the goal, and run the next round from a cleaner starting point. The AIs will refine what's there — they won't reintroduce content you removed, especially once your goal explicitly excludes it.

Option C — Use conflicts as they surface

If the drift is showing up as conflicts (AIs disagreeing about out-of-scope content), handle it right there. Write a Custom resolution with the corrected text, or Decline to suppress the suggestion. Once resolved, it's locked — the hive won't re-raise it.

Option D — Start over

If the document has drifted badly enough that correcting it round by round would take longer than starting fresh, use Clear Project on the Project screen. This clears the document and round history but keeps your API keys. Rewrite your goal with the tighter scope and generate a new first draft.

Starting over isn't a failure — sometimes a well-written second goal produces a better round-one document than five rounds of correcting a draft built on a weak goal.

Using Notes Effectively
Notes go directly to the Builder

The Notes field on the work screen lets you inject one-time instructions into the Builder's prompt for the next round only. After the Builder runs, the notes clear automatically. This makes them ideal for targeted corrections, one-off redirections, or anything you don't want to permanently encode into the goal.

Good uses for notes

Scope corrections — "Remove all references to switching and routing. This document covers wireless only."

Tone adjustments — "Tighten the language throughout. This is a technical document — cut any marketing-style phrasing."

Structural changes — "Move the pricing section to after the feature comparison. Add a summary table at the end."

One-time additions — "Add a brief note in the intro acknowledging that all pricing is subject to change and should be verified with each vendor."

Notes are powerful but single-use. If you find yourself typing the same note every round, that's a signal — it belongs in your goal instead.

Knowing When You're Done
Watch for convergence signals

The hive tells you when the document is maturing. The clearest signal: multiple AIs returning NO CHANGES in the same round. When that starts happening, the reviewers are running out of meaningful suggestions — the document has converged.

Other signals: conflicts become less frequent, the suggestions get smaller and more stylistic, and the document starts looking like something you'd actually send.

You're always in charge of the finish line

There's no magic number of rounds. Some documents are done in 3, some take 10. The hive will keep going as long as you keep running rounds — but after convergence, you'll get diminishing returns. Trust the signals, do a final read, and hit Finish when it looks right to you.

Before finishing, toggle off AIs that have been returning NO CHANGES for a couple of rounds and run one final round with just your most critical reviewers. This often produces the cleanest final pass.

Export before clearing

When you hit Finish, WaxFrame will prompt you to export your document before starting a new project — your working document and round history will be cleared when you do. Export as a .txt file, complete with a project byline showing how many rounds it took and how long the session ran. You can also export a Full Transcript — a single file containing every round, every AI response, and the final document — from the History panel or the Finish screen.
